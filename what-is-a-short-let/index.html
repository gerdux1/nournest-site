What is a short let?

Definition, how long it lasts, and how short lets differ from holiday lets, hotels and Airbnb.

Quick answer

A short let is a furnished residential rental for between one night and 90 nights, let by the owner or a managing agent. In London, the legal cap for whole-home short lets without planning permission is 90 nights per calendar year. Short lets are taxed under residential rather than commercial rules in most jurisdictions, and sit between hotel stays and assured shorthold tenancies on the rental length spectrum.

The simple definition

A short let is a furnished residential rental for a brief period — typically one to 90 nights — let by the property owner or a managing agent. It's the residential equivalent of a hotel stay, but in a home rather than a room.

Short lets include the furniture, kitchenware, linens, towels, wifi and utilities. The guest arrives with luggage and leaves the same way.

How long is a short let?

Most short lets are between three nights and three months. In London specifically:

  • 1-90 nights: classified as a short let. Whole-home short lets are capped at 90 nights per calendar year without planning permission (the so-called "90-day rule" introduced by the Deregulation Act 2015).
  • 91-179 nights: medium-term let. Different tax and planning treatment.
  • 180+ nights: typically an Assured Shorthold Tenancy (AST) — fully residential, deposit-protected, six-month minimum.

NourNest apartments take guests for any length within the short-let band (3 nights to 90 nights), with monthly rates applying from 28 nights.

Where short lets are common in London

Central London (Mayfair, Marylebone, Kensington, Pimlico), the City fringes (Shoreditch, Hoxton, Aldgate, Old Street), and the King's Cross / Russell Square axis are the most popular short-let neighbourhoods — driven by business travel, tourism, and project stays.
